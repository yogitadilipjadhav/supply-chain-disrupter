DISRUPTION PROFILE: HIGH-BANDWIDTH MEMORY (HBM) SHORTAGE — AI CHIP DEMAND SURGE (2023–2024)
SEVERITY: HIGH | CATEGORY: Demand Shock | GEOGRAPHY: South Korea (SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron); Taiwan (TSMC CoWoS packaging)
PRIMARY IMPACT: AI accelerator production (NVIDIA, AMD, Google TPU); cloud computing capacity; AI infrastructure buildout

Beginning in mid-2023, the explosive growth in demand for generative AI infrastructure created a severe shortage of High Bandwidth Memory (HBM), the specialized memory chips used in AI accelerators including NVIDIA's H100, H200, and later Blackwell GPUs, AMD's MI300X, and Google's TPU v4/v5 series. HBM represents a qualitatively different semiconductor supply chain constraint from conventional DRAM shortages because HBM production requires mastery of advanced 3D stacking technology (Through-Silicon Via, TSV), is produced in limited quantities, and must be integrated with GPU dies using specialized advanced packaging processes (TSMC CoWoS — Chip on Wafer on Substrate).

SK Hynix entered 2023 as effectively the only company with production-ready HBM3 (the third generation) capable of meeting NVIDIA's H100 certification requirements, giving SK Hynix a near-monopoly on the highest-value memory segment for AI applications. Samsung HBM3 failed to pass NVIDIA's quality and performance certification for H100 through most of 2023-early 2024, leaving it excluded from the most critical AI customer. Micron Technology entered the HBM3E (fourth generation) market in 2024 as a qualified supplier for NVIDIA H200, becoming the third HBM supplier, but with limited initial volumes.

TSMC's CoWoS packaging capacity became the binding constraint limiting the entire NVIDIA GPU-HBM complex's production volume. CoWoS is required to physically integrate (through interposer and substrate stacking) the GPU compute die (manufactured by TSMC at 4nm) with 4-8 HBM stacks to form the complete AI accelerator package. TSMC's CoWoS capacity was effectively sold out for 18+ months, with allocation backlogs stretching through 2024 and into 2025. This meant that even if SK Hynix had unlimited HBM supply, total AI accelerator production was bottlenecked by TSMC's ability to attach HBM to GPU dies in CoWoS packages.

ECONOMIC SCALE: NVIDIA's H100 GPU sold for $25,000-40,000 per unit in 2023, with hyperscaler customers (Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Amazon AWS, Meta) paying $40,000+ including server integration. HBM accounts for approximately 20-25% of the H100's bill of materials at $100-120 per HBM3 stack (4-6 stacks per H100), making HBM among the highest-value memory products ever produced, at roughly 20-30x the per-byte price of conventional DRAM. NVIDIA's data center revenue grew from $3.6B in Q1 2023 to $18.4B in Q1 2024, driven almost entirely by H100 AI accelerator demand.

DOWNSTREAM IMPACT ON ELECTRONICS SUPPLY CHAINS: The HBM/AI demand surge created secondary supply chain effects: (1) DRAM CAPEX shifted toward HBM production lines, reducing conventional DRAM capacity and causing server DRAM price increases; (2) Samsung's factory resources diverted toward HBM certification created delays in conventional foundry commitments; (3) Advanced packaging capacity competition between HBM/CoWoS work and other advanced packaging customers (chiplets, 3D stacking for non-AI applications).

SUPPLY CHAIN PLANNING IMPLICATIONS: For electronics supply chain demand forecasting, the 2023-24 HBM shortage demonstrates that AI-driven demand surges can create concentrated capacity constraints in narrow semiconductor categories that cascade across the broader memory market. Risk Classification should flag any signals of AI infrastructure hyperscaler ordering surges (NVIDIA quarterly revenue beats, cloud CapEx guidance increases) as early indicators of potential memory allocation pressure on consumer electronics memory (smartphone DRAM, NAND).
