DISRUPTION PROFILE: MV EVER GIVEN SUEZ CANAL BLOCKAGE (MARCH 23–29, 2021)
SEVERITY: HIGH | CATEGORY: Logistics Chokepoint, Single-Event Shock | GEOGRAPHY: Suez Canal, Ismailia, Egypt
PRIMARY IMPACT: Asia-Europe trade lane; electronics components, commodities, energy, consumer goods

The MV Ever Given, a 400-meter container ship operated by Evergreen Marine Corporation (Taiwan), ran aground diagonally across the Suez Canal on March 23, 2021, completely blocking the waterway for six days until refloating was achieved on March 29, 2021. The blockage at the southern section of the canal near Ismailia represented one of the most disruptive single maritime events in modern supply chain history.

At its peak, approximately 369 ships were queued at both the northern (Mediterranean) and southern (Red Sea) ends of the canal. The Suez Canal handles approximately 12 percent of global trade and 30 percent of global container traffic, making it a critical chokepoint for Asia-Europe supply chains. Estimates from Lloyd's List placed the value of trade held up during the blockage at approximately $9.6 billion per day. Over the full six-day period, total impacted cargo value exceeded $54 billion.

For electronics supply chains specifically, the Ever Given blockage demonstrated how even a short-duration (six-day) single-point disruption creates ripple effects lasting 4-6 weeks. Component shipments from Taiwanese, South Korean, Chinese, and Southeast Asian manufacturers destined for European assembly plants and distribution centers were delayed by 8-15 days on average, as ships waited in queue or made partial Cape of Good Hope diversions. The disruption coincided with the broader 2021 global chip shortage, compounding lead time pressure on European automotive and consumer electronics OEMs.

Insurance market response was significant. Lloyd's Market Association estimated industry exposure at $2-3 billion in marine insurance claims and contingent business interruption claims. War risk premiums for the Red Sea corridor were not triggered (unlike the 2023-24 Houthi attacks) since this was an accident rather than a security threat, but delay clauses in shipping contracts were widely invoked.

Unlike the 2023-24 Houthi crisis, the Ever Given blockage was too short in duration to trigger mass Cape of Good Hope diversions — the economic calculation did not favor adding 10-14 days and $400,000-600,000 in additional fuel costs per voyage for a six-day blockage. Most ships queued and waited rather than diverting. This decision logic itself became a lesson: firms that had built 10+ days of safety stock absorbed the delay without stockouts; firms operating on just-in-time inventory at 5-7 days experienced stockouts within two weeks.

The Egyptian Suez Canal Authority (SCA) temporarily impounded the Ever Given pending a $916 million compensation claim from Egypt covering salvage costs, lost canal revenue, and reputational damages. After extended negotiations, Evergreen Marine reached a settlement (believed to be in the range of $200-300 million) in June 2021, and the ship was released.

COMPARISON TO RED SEA 2023-24: The Ever Given blockage was a single accidental event resolved in 6 days; the Houthi crisis was a sustained security threat running 12+ months. The insurance mechanism differed (marine delay vs. war risk). The routing response differed (queue-and-wait vs. Cape diversion). The persistent nature of the Houthi threat made it structurally more damaging to supply chain planning.

MITIGATION LESSONS: (1) Maintaining 10-14 days of safety stock on high-value, time-sensitive electronics components provides a buffer for short-duration chokepoint events; (2) Pre-negotiated Cape of Good Hope rerouting options with key carriers enable faster activation if a blockage extends beyond 3 days; (3) Single chokepoint dependency (100% of trade through one canal) should be flagged as CRITICAL infrastructure risk in supplier contracts; (4) Real-time AIS (Automatic Identification System) vessel tracking enables 24-48 hour advance warning of chokepoint congestion.
