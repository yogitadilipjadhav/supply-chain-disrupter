DISRUPTION PROFILE: CHINA CRITICAL MINERAL EXPORT CONTROLS — GALLIUM AND GERMANIUM (2023–2024)
SEVERITY: HIGH | CATEGORY: Geopolitical, Export Control, Raw Material | GEOGRAPHY: China (primary production); US, Japan, Europe (consuming markets)
PRIMARY IMPACT: GaN and GaAs semiconductor production; SiGe chips; fiber optics; infrared optics; EV power electronics

China's Ministry of Commerce (MOFCOM) announced on July 3, 2023 that gallium and germanium would be subject to export licensing controls effective August 1, 2023, under MOFCOM Order numbers 13 and 14 of 2023. This was widely interpreted as a retaliatory measure in response to US semiconductor export controls announced in October 2022 and expanded in October 2023, as well as coordinated allied controls by Japan and the Netherlands. China controls approximately 80 percent of global primary gallium production and approximately 60 percent of global germanium production, giving it significant leverage over users of these materials in semiconductor manufacturing.

GALLIUM AND ITS SEMICONDUCTOR USES: Gallium is the primary input for gallium arsenide (GaAs) and gallium nitride (GaN) semiconductors. GaAs chips are used in RF front-end modules for smartphones (including 5G mmWave), satellite communications, radar systems, and high-efficiency solar cells. GaN semiconductors are critical for power electronics in EV inverters, base station power amplifiers, fast chargers, and data center power conversion. A modern 5G smartphone contains GaAs or GaN chips from RF chipset makers including Skyworks, Qorvo, Broadcom, and Murata. Any supply disruption for gallium flows directly to reduced production capacity for 5G RF chips and EV power modules — both high-growth semiconductor categories.

GERMANIUM AND ITS SEMICONDUCTOR USES: Germanium is used in silicon germanium (SiGe) heterojunction bipolar transistors (HBTs) for high-frequency analog circuits; in germanium-based transistors for space applications (high radiation tolerance); as a substrate for high-efficiency multi-junction solar cells; in optical fiber preforms as a dopant; and in infrared optical systems (night vision, thermal imaging). SiGe chips from IBM, NXP, and Infineon are used in automotive radar, 77GHz automotive ADAS systems, and 5G front-end modules.

MARKET IMPACT: Gallium spot prices approximately doubled in the months following the July 2023 announcement, rising from roughly $220 per kilogram to above $400 per kilogram. Germanium prices rose approximately 50 percent. Companies with significant gallium inventory (3-6 month supply) were insulated from immediate impact; those on just-in-time resupply began experiencing allocation constraints within 60-90 days of the controls taking effect. The controls required Chinese exporters to apply for licenses with no automatic approval — the uncertainty itself drove inventory hoarding and price spikes.

ESCALATION — GRAPHITE CONTROLS (OCTOBER 2023): China expanded its critical mineral export controls to natural graphite in October 2023. China controls approximately 65 percent of global natural graphite production and over 90 percent of synthetic graphite anode material production. Graphite anodes are a critical component of lithium-ion battery cells used in EVs and consumer electronics. The electronics-semiconductor linkage: graphite is used in semiconductor manufacturing equipment electrodes, EDM tooling, and as a process material in semiconductor furnaces.

ESCALATION — ANTIMONY CONTROLS (2024): China added antimony to export control lists in 2024. Antimony is used in flame retardants for electronics PCBs, semiconductor dopants, and in lead-acid batteries. China controls approximately 50-60 percent of global antimony production.

MITIGATION STRATEGIES: (1) Strategic inventory accumulation: firms with 6-month+ gallium inventory hedged price spike risk; (2) Alternate source development: Japanese producers (Dowa Holdings) and German producers (Chemetall) ramped capacity, though China's cost advantage is significant; (3) Material efficiency: GaN chip manufacturers explored design optimizations reducing gallium content per wafer; (4) Substitution: For some applications, silicon-based power devices (SiC, Si IGBT) can substitute for GaN in some voltage ranges; (5) Government strategic reserves: DoE and METI began programs to establish national gallium/germanium reserves.
