DISRUPTION PROFILE: UKRAINE WAR — SEMICONDUCTOR-GRADE NEON AND SPECIALTY GAS CRISIS (2022)
SEVERITY: HIGH | CATEGORY: Geopolitical, Raw Material Supply | GEOGRAPHY: Ukraine (Mariupol, Odessa); Russia
PRIMARY IMPACT: Semiconductor photolithography; leading-edge chip production (TSMC, Samsung, Intel, SK Hynix)

The Russian invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022 triggered an acute risk to global semiconductor manufacturing through disruption of specialty gas supply chains, most critically semiconductor-grade neon. Ukraine, through two companies — Ingas (headquartered in Mariupol) and Cryoin Engineering (headquartered in Odessa) — supplied approximately 45 to 54 percent of the world's semiconductor-grade neon, according to the US Department of Commerce Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) findings from the 2021 Semiconductor Supply Chain Request for Information. This neon dependency had been identified as a critical vulnerability in the DoC/BIS RFI report published in January 2022 — just weeks before the invasion materialized the identified risk.

Neon gas plays an indispensable role in semiconductor photolithography. It is used as the excimer laser medium in argon-fluoride (ArF) deep ultraviolet (DUV) lasers, which are the primary light source for photolithography systems at 28nm to 7nm technology nodes — the nodes responsible for the majority of global semiconductor unit production. Without continuous neon supply, DUV lithography tools (manufactured by ASML, Nikon, and Canon) cannot operate, directly halting wafer fabrication.

Both Ukrainian neon suppliers temporarily suspended operations in the early weeks of the conflict. Ingas's Mariupol facility became inaccessible due to military operations in the city. Cryoin in Odessa reduced and then suspended exports. Spot market neon prices responded immediately, surging from approximately $630 per thousand cubic feet in early 2022 to above $3,200 per thousand cubic feet by March 2022 — an increase exceeding 400 percent. At its peak in March-April 2022, neon prices had risen approximately five times from pre-war levels.

BROADER GAS DEPENDENCY: The neon crisis was part of a wider specialty gas vulnerability. Ukraine and Russia combined supplied significant fractions of several other semiconductor process gases: krypton (used in 248nm KrF lasers); xenon; and hexafluorobutadiene (C4F6 / octafluorocyclobutane), used in plasma etching of silicon dioxide layers. Russia was also a significant supplier of palladium (used in ceramic chip capacitors and catalytic converters). The US DoC/BIS 2021 RFI had specifically flagged that the two Ukrainian neon suppliers and Russia's specialty gas industry represented concentrated single-source dependencies.

MARKET ADAPTATION AND RECOVERY: The semiconductor industry proved more resilient than initial alarm suggested, for several reasons. Major fabs had accumulated 2-6 month neon inventories as a precautionary measure following the 2014 Crimea annexation — the first event that highlighted Ukrainian neon dependency. South Korean neon producers (POSCO, Linde Korea) rapidly scaled output. Chinese neon production, though lower purity, was expanded. By Q3 2022, alternative supply chains had largely normalized neon availability for leading semiconductor manufacturers, though at structurally higher cost. Neon prices fell from peak levels but did not return to pre-war prices through 2023.

SUPPLY CHAIN LESSONS: (1) Single-geography raw material dependency for critical process inputs (even seemingly commodity gases) creates semiconductor production vulnerability; (2) The 2014 Crimea annexation created awareness but not sufficient diversification — a pattern where supply chain risks are identified but not remediated until a disruption event occurs; (3) Fabs with 6-month+ specialty gas inventories were insulated; fabs on lean replenishment were exposed; (4) Substitute sourcing for neon was possible within 3-6 months (unlike semiconductors themselves, where no substitutes exist); (5) Geopolitical intelligence — monitoring Ukraine-Russia tensions provided a 6+ month warning before supply disruption, enabling pre-positioning inventory.
