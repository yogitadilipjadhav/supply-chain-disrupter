REGULATION PROFILE: CHIPS AND SCIENCE ACT — US DOMESTIC SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING INCENTIVES (2022)
SIGNED: August 9, 2022 | CITATION: Public Law 117-167 | ADMINISTERING AGENCY: US Department of Commerce (NIST, BIS)
TOTAL AUTHORIZATION: $52.7 billion | EXPORT_CONTROL_LEVEL: HIGH (national security guardrails on recipients)

The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 is the US government's primary policy response to the structural vulnerabilities in the global semiconductor supply chain revealed by the 2020-2021 chip shortage, US-China technology competition, and Taiwan concentration risk. The legislation provides direct subsidies, tax credits, and R&D funding to incentivize semiconductor manufacturing in the United States, while imposing national security conditions on recipients.

SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING INCENTIVES — $39 BILLION: The largest component provides direct financial assistance for establishing, expanding, or modernizing semiconductor manufacturing facilities in the United States. Eligibility covers wafer fabrication, OSAT (assembly, test, packaging), and semiconductor manufacturing equipment. The Commerce Department administers applications through the CHIPS Program Office.

Notable CHIPS Act Awards (announced 2024): TSMC Arizona received up to $6.6 billion in direct CHIPS funding plus $5 billion in Commerce Department loans for its planned three-fab complex in Phoenix (N4P/4nm Phase 1, N3/3nm Phase 2, likely 2nm Phase 3). Intel received up to $8.5 billion in direct funding plus $11 billion in loan authority for facilities in Arizona (Intel 18A node), Ohio (planned 2nm+ mega-fab), Oregon, and New Mexico. Samsung Electronics received up to $6.4 billion for its Taylor, Texas facility expansion (4nm foundry). Micron Technology received up to $6.1 billion for DRAM manufacturing expansion in Boise, Idaho and Clay, New York (HBM-capable). GlobalFoundries received up to $1.5 billion for its Maltese, New York and Burlington, Vermont facilities (mature nodes: 12nm and above for automotive, defense, IoT).

INVESTMENT TAX CREDIT — 25% (SECTION 48D): A 25 percent advanced manufacturing investment tax credit for qualified semiconductor manufacturing facility investments, including both equipment and construction costs. This credit is in addition to the direct incentive grants, and is available to fabs building wafer manufacturing, semiconductor manufacturing equipment manufacturing, or OSAT facilities.

NATIONAL SECURITY GUARDRAILS — CRITICAL SUPPLY CHAIN RESTRICTION: CHIPS Act recipients are prohibited from using funds to significantly expand semiconductor manufacturing capacity in China or other countries of concern (Russia, North Korea, Iran) for a period of 10 years from the grant award date. "Significant expansion" is defined as capacity additions beyond a de minimis threshold (roughly 5% capacity increase for leading-edge chips, 10% for mature-node legacy chips). This guardrail is enforceable through clawback provisions: CHIPS grant recipients that violate the guardrail must return all granted funds with interest. This guardrail directly affects TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron — all of which have existing manufacturing operations in China. Each company committed to limiting China capacity expansion as a condition of receiving CHIPS Act funding.

R&D PROVISIONS — $11 BILLION: The National Semiconductor Technology Center (NSTC), established by CHIPS, provides a hub for semiconductor R&D at sub-3nm nodes, workforce training, and prototyping capabilities accessible to US-based designers and manufacturers without access to leading-edge foundry capacity.

WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT — $2.7 BILLION: Addresses the estimated 90,000-person skilled workforce gap (technicians, engineers, process specialists) identified as a constraint on US semiconductor manufacturing expansion. Programs target community college semiconductor technician training and university-level semiconductor engineering graduate programs.

SUPPLY CHAIN PLANNING IMPLICATIONS: For orders involving CHIPS-funded facilities (TSMC Arizona, Samsung Taylor, Intel Arizona/Ohio, Micron Idaho/New York), procurement planners can assume US-based production with reduced Taiwan concentration risk, but must account for: (1) US labor costs 25-50% higher than Taiwan equivalent; (2) Ramp timelines 6-12 months longer than historical for first-of-kind US fab operations; (3) US-produced chips are not subject to Taiwan weather/earthquake/geopolitical risk scoring, warranting lower `Natural_Disaster_Risk` and `Export_Control_Level` scores compared to equivalent Taiwan-produced chips.
