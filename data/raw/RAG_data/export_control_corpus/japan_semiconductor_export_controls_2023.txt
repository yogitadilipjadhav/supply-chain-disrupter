REGULATION PROFILE: JAPAN SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING EQUIPMENT EXPORT CONTROLS (JULY 2023)
EFFECTIVE DATE: July 23, 2023 | ISSUER: Japan Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI)
REGULATORY BASIS: Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act (FEFTA) amendments; Export Trade Control Order
EXPORT_CONTROL_LEVEL: HIGH | GEOGRAPHY: China (primary target); no ally exemptions specified initially

Japan became the second member of the emerging semiconductor export control coalition (alongside the United States and Netherlands) to implement significant restrictions on semiconductor manufacturing equipment exports, effective July 23, 2023. Japan's controls targeted 23 categories of semiconductor manufacturing equipment, aiming to prevent China's semiconductor manufacturers (SMIC, Hua Hong, CXMT) from acquiring Japanese equipment needed to advance their manufacturing capabilities to sub-14nm technology nodes.

The 23 controlled categories include: advanced film deposition equipment (atomic layer deposition, chemical vapor deposition for thin films below certain thickness specifications); plasma etching systems capable of high-aspect-ratio silicon etching for FinFET and advanced 3D NAND structures; certain 193nm immersion lithography tools configured for multi-patterning below 14nm; ion implantation systems for advanced doping profiles; and measurement and inspection equipment with sub-nanometer resolution capabilities. The controls do not specifically target EUV lithography (already handled through Netherlands/ASML's export license revocation policy), but cover the DUV-era toolset required for SMIC to advance its N+2/N+1 process development.

AFFECTED JAPANESE EQUIPMENT COMPANIES: The controls directly impact Japan's leading semiconductor equipment manufacturers: Tokyo Electron (TEL) — the world's second-largest semiconductor equipment company (after Applied Materials), supplying etch, CVD, and track systems critical for advanced node manufacturing. Shin-Etsu Chemical — leading silicon wafer and photomask blank supplier. Sumco Corporation — silicon wafer manufacturer. Kokusai Electric — batch thermal processing equipment. Lasertec Corporation — EUV and DUV mask inspection systems. These companies had generated significant China revenue (China represented 30-40% of TEL's revenue in 2022), and the controls created a meaningful reduction in addressable market for their advanced equipment lines.

TRILATERAL COORDINATION (US-JAPAN-NETHERLANDS): Japan's July 2023 controls were part of coordinated policy with the United States (October 2022 BIS controls, expanded October 2023) and the Netherlands (ASML export license restrictions). The three countries collectively control the manufacturing of virtually all advanced semiconductor production equipment: the US dominates CVD, ALD, etch, and ion implant equipment (Applied Materials, Lam Research, Axcelis, Mattson); the Netherlands dominates advanced lithography through ASML (monopoly on EUV, dominant in immersion DUV); Japan dominates certain CVD, etch, track, and wafer equipment (Tokyo Electron, Advantest for test equipment, Hitachi High-Tech for metrology). China cannot build advanced semiconductor fabs at 7nm and below without equipment from all three of these country blocs.

OSAKA SUPPLY CHAIN MONITORING NOTE: For the Weather Risk Agent monitoring Osaka as a semiconductor hub city, Japan's export controls create a regulatory dimension to Osaka-region supply chain monitoring beyond weather risk. Tokyo Electron, Shin-Etsu, and Sumco have significant operations in the greater Kanto/Osaka corridor. Any disruption to these companies' operations — whether from weather events, regulatory changes, or factory incidents — affects the global supply of controlled equipment and materials.

RISK CLASSIFICATION GUIDANCE: Transactions involving Japanese semiconductor manufacturing equipment destined for Chinese fabs, or involving Japanese-produced chips fabricated using controlled equipment configurations, should carry elevated Export_Control_Level scores for years 2023 onward. The Japan controls create a complementary regulatory risk layer alongside the BIS controls, meaning that China-sourced electronics supply chains face compounding export control risks from US AND Japanese regulatory frameworks simultaneously.
