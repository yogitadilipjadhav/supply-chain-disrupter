POLICY ANALYSIS: CRS SEMICONDUCTOR REPORTS — US INDUSTRY, POLICY, AND EXPORT CONTROLS
SOURCE REPORTS: R46581 (2021), R47508 (2022), R47558 (2023), R48642 (2024)
EXPORT_CONTROL_LEVEL: HIGH | CATEGORY: Policy Analysis, Industry Structure, Regulatory Framework

REPORT R46581 — "SEMICONDUCTORS: U.S. INDUSTRY, GLOBAL COMPETITION, AND FEDERAL POLICY" (2021):
This CRS report provides the foundational policy analysis of the US semiconductor industry's competitive position and the structural vulnerabilities exposed by the 2020-2021 chip shortage. Key findings: The US share of global semiconductor manufacturing capacity declined from approximately 37 percent in 1990 to approximately 12 percent in 2020, driven by lower fab construction and operating costs in Asia (estimated 25-50% cost advantage for Taiwan and South Korea over US-equivalent facilities, primarily due to government subsidies in host countries and lower labor costs for technicians and engineers). Despite this manufacturing decline, the US remains dominant in semiconductor design (approximately 47% of global chip design revenue) and in semiconductor manufacturing equipment (approximately 50% of global market). The US also dominates Electronic Design Automation (EDA) software, with Synopsys, Cadence, and Mentor (acquired by Siemens) controlling approximately 75% of global EDA revenue. This design-and-equipment dominance provides the leverage used in export control frameworks, since even non-US manufactured chips often rely on US-origin design tools and equipment.

The report analyzed the strategic risk of Taiwan concentration: at the time of writing, TSMC produced approximately 92% of the world's most advanced chips (7nm and below), creating a single point of failure for the entire global electronics industry. A disruption to TSMC's operations — from earthquake, military conflict, or other cause — would affect virtually every sector that uses electronics, including defense, automotive, medical devices, and consumer technology.

REPORT R47508 — "CHIPS ACT: SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING INCENTIVES" (2022):
Analyzes the $39 billion semiconductor manufacturing incentives in the CHIPS and Science Act, including subsidy structure, guardrail requirements, application process, and economic rationale. The report estimates that building leading-edge fab capacity in the US costs 25-50% more than in Taiwan or South Korea after accounting for labor, land, construction, and utilities — making the CHIPS Act subsidies necessary but not sufficient to achieve cost parity without ongoing government support. The "leading-edge" definition for priority funding is chips manufactured at 7nm or below (or equivalent using non-planar transistor architectures). The report notes that the 10-year China expansion guardrail creates legal obligations that CHIPS recipients must monitor continuously, including through supply chain due diligence for joint ventures and technology licensing agreements.

REPORT R47558 — "EXPORT CONTROLS: BIS RULES ON ADVANCED SEMICONDUCTORS AND MANUFACTURING EQUIPMENT" (2023):
This report provides the most detailed CRS analysis of the October 2022 and October 2023 BIS export control rules, including legal authorities, affected entities, and compliance requirements. Key analysis: The Foreign Direct Product Rule (FDPR) for Advanced Computing extends US jurisdiction to foreign-manufactured items that are products of US-origin technology or software, effectively enabling US export controls to reach non-US companies operating outside US territory that use US technology in their production processes. The report discusses compliance challenges for multinational semiconductor companies: a Korean chip designer using US EDA tools to design a chip manufactured at TSMC using US-origin deposition equipment is subject to US export controls when selling those chips to a Chinese buyer exceeding the computing performance thresholds. The report also analyzes the Entity List mechanism, noting that the presumption of denial for Entity List transactions effectively creates a near-complete embargo on exports to listed Chinese semiconductor companies.

REPORT R48642 — "SEMICONDUCTOR SUPPLY CHAIN SECURITY: RECENT DEVELOPMENTS" (2024):
The most recent CRS report in the series addresses the implementation progress of the CHIPS Act and the emergence of allied coordination in semiconductor export controls. Key findings: TSMC Arizona Fab 21 Phase 1 (N4P, 4nm) experienced initial delays due to skilled worker shortages — TSMC temporarily relocated hundreds of Taiwanese technicians to Arizona to train US personnel, triggering scrutiny from Arizona labor unions. Intel's Ohio fabs (Fab 52 and 54) experienced construction delays. The Chip4/Fab4 Alliance (US, Taiwan, Japan, South Korea) emerged as an informal coordination mechanism for semiconductor supply chain resilience, focusing on inventory data sharing, coordinated export control policy, and joint R&D investment. China's SMIC reported achieving N+1 node production (approximately equivalent to 7nm) in volume production by 2023-2024, demonstrating that export controls slowed but did not halt China's semiconductor advancement — raising questions about the long-term effectiveness of the control regime versus investment in allied supply chain depth.

UNIFIED POLICY IMPLICATIONS FOR RISK CLASSIFICATION: The CRS reports collectively establish that Export_Control_Level scoring should account for: (1) Whether chip design used US-origin EDA tools (virtually all advanced chips — triggers FDPR jurisdiction); (2) Whether chips manufactured at US-controlled technology nodes (7nm and below for leading-edge controls, broader nodes for SME controls); (3) Whether buyer or end-user is on BIS Entity List; (4) Whether shipment destination is China, Macau, or other countries of concern; (5) For China-sourced chips at mature nodes (40nm+), control risk is lower but still present for military end-use applications.
